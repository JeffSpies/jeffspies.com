Intro

The basics.

By education, I am a computer scientist, statistician, and psychologist. In practice, I am a research technologist and methodologist with a focus on workflow, incentives, and biases. I am currently most motivated by increasing research efficiency. That includes increasing research quality, accessibilty, and diversity.

I am the co-founder and Chief Technology Officer (CTO) of the Center for Open Science (COS), a non-profit technology company missioned to increase openness, integrity, and reproducibility of scholarly research. As CTO, I am responsible for organizational strategy with a focus on the technical, product vision, the software architecture and technical stack of COS products and services, external partner and funder development, and management of COS Labs—COS's research and development team. I also co-lead SHARE—an initiative by the Association of Research Libraries and COS to create a free, open dataset of research activity across the research life-cycle. I have a Ph.D. in Quantitative Psychology (read: Behavioral Statistics) from the University of Virginia, where I now hold a Visiting Assistant Professor position in the Department of Engineering and Society. My dissertation included the development of the Open Science Framework—a free, open source workflow management system and scholarly commons that is now the flagship product of COS.

Hats

I wear a few.
01

Entrepreneur

In 2013, I founded COS with Brian Nosek. To date, we have raised $26M in funding from a variety of sources. Our flagship product, the Open Science Framework (OSF), was my dissertation project.

02

Technologist

I'm the CTO at COS, where I am responsible for organizational strategy with a focus on the technical, product vision, the software architecture and technical stack of COS products and services, external partner and funder development, and management of COS Labs—COS's research and development team.

03

Researcher and Scientist

In practice, I focus more on building tools in order to help others do their science more efficiently than doing research myself. When I was actively doing research, I studied topics including information visualization, time-series analysis, autism, non-verbal communication, and motor control.

04

Librarian

OK, not technically, but I co-lead the SHARE project, a partnership between COS and the Association of Research Libraries, and spend a lot of time with real librarians. I like to think that I can talk digital archival, preservation, and metadata with the best of them (I probably can't, but I like to think that). I believe the library is key to improving scholarship and should play a central role on campuses, especially with regard to increasing innovation and diversity.

05

Openness Advocate

Open source, open science, open scholarship, open data, open workflow—open solves many of the challenges currently recognized in the sciences and scholarship more broadly and, more than that, makes research more efficient, of higher quality, and more accessible and diverse.

06

Educator

I have taught both undergraduates and graduate courses (centered around statistics and/or computing) and a number of workshops. I also created COS's internship program through which we've trained 127 technical interns and hired 29. The internship program is based upon a pedagogy I developed at Notre Dame. The goal of the pedagogy is to achieve increased learning efficiency with a focus on techniques to attract and retain minorities in STEM (e.g., real-world projects, service-learning setting, particular attention paid to acknowledging imposter syndrome and positively interpreting frustration). COS has more work to do, but 35% of our interns are women, and we've hired at that exact same rate. Engineering can and needs to be far more diverse than it is.
